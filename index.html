§ 1 · Introduction
Section One

The vector is the connective tissue of machine intelligence.

AI's evolution from rule-based symbolic systems to deep learning rests on a single, often invisible, mathematical object — the vector.

  • Encodes meaning — images, words, sounds become coordinates.
  • Stores knowledge — a network's weights are themselves vectors.
  • Enables reasoning — geometry replaces symbolic rules.

“In high-dimensional geometry, intelligence is not a program — it is a position.”

§ 2 · Historical Context
From Perceptron to High Dimension

A neuron is a hyperplane in disguise.

Rosenblatt's 1958 Perceptron treated a single neuron as a line cutting space in two — a linear classifier living on a vector space.

The decision rule
$$ \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} + b \;\gtrless\; 0 $$

The AI Winter (1970s) arrived when researchers proved that a single linear cut cannot separate the XOR classes — the insight that eventually birthed multi-layer, "Deep" networks.

§ 3 · Data Representation
Vectorization

Reality, projected into coordinates.

Before a network can think, the world must become numbers.

§ 4 · Architecture of Learning
Weights, dot products, descent

Each weight vector is a template; learning is a walk downhill.

A neuron, geometrically
$$ z = \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} + b, \qquad a = \varphi(z) $$

The dot product measures alignment — how much $\mathbf{x}$ projects onto the stored pattern $\mathbf{w}$.

Gradient descent in a million-dimensional landscape
$$ \mathbf{w}_{t+1} = \mathbf{w}_{t} - \eta\,\nabla_{\mathbf{w}}\mathcal{L} $$

The gradient is itself a vector pointing uphill; we step against it, again and again.

§ 5 · Latent Spaces & the Manifold Hypothesis
Where data really lives

High-D data lies on a low-D manifold.

Pixels live in millions of dimensions, but the set of natural images forms a thin curved surface inside that volume.

  • Early layers learn edges and textures.
  • Deep layers learn abstract concepts.
  • Together they "unroll" the manifold so a simple classifier can finish the job.

“Activation functions warp the page; deep networks crumple paper until the dots fall apart.”

§ 6 · Orthogonality & Cosine Similarity
Why high dimensions are spacious

Two random vectors in high-D are almost orthogonal.

That near-90° property is what lets a single network store vast amounts of information without interference — unrelated concepts simply don't see one another.

Cosine similarity
$$ \cos\theta \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert} $$

Small angle → high similarity. Right angle → no relationship at all.

θ ≈ 0° · synonyms θ ≈ 90° · independent θ ≈ 180° · opposites
Figure 8 · Training pulls related vectors together (small θ) and pushes unrelated ones apart (large θ).

§ 7 · Convolution & Tensors
From vectors to volumes

CNNs slide a small weight pattern across an image.

The input is no longer a 1-D vector but a 3-D tensor (width × height × channels). The weight vector becomes a kernel — a tiny template that produces a localized dot product at every spatial position.

  • Feature maps reveal where a pattern occurs.
  • Pooling compresses location into existence.
  • By the final layer, the spatial image is a single abstract feature vector.

§ 8 · Transformers — Attention
The decade's biggest leap

Attention is just weighted dot products.

Every token projects itself three ways:

  • Q — what am I looking for?
  • K — what do I contain?
  • V — what do I pass on?
Scaled dot-product attention
$$ \text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$

The result is a dynamic embedding — a word's vector shifts position depending on the rest of the sentence.

§ 9 · Memory — Vector Databases
Long-term memory for AI

Search by meaning, not by characters.

Traditional databases match keywords. Vector databases embed every document as a point and answer queries by nearest-neighbour search in that space.

  • Question is embedded into the same space.
  • Closest vectors are returned by cosine or Euclidean distance.
  • Powers retrieval-augmented generation, semantic search, recommendation.

“Geometry replaces grep — proximity becomes relevance.”

A query is embedded into the same space; the database returns its nearest neighbours.

§ 10 · The Curse of Dimensionality
A peril of high-D space

In high dimensions, everything is far — and almost on the edge.

As the number of dimensions grows, the volume of space explodes. Data becomes sparse; pairwise distances concentrate; "nearness" loses meaning.

§ 11 · Normalization & Vector Magnitude
Direction over intensity

Normalize the magnitude — let the direction carry the meaning.

If a vector grows too large, activation functions saturate, gradients vanish, learning stops. Constraining vectors to a unit sphere forces the network to focus on where the vector points, not how loud it shouts.

L₂ normalization
$$ \hat{\mathbf{x}} \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\lVert\mathbf{x}\rVert_2} $$
Softmax — turning a vector into a probability
$$ \mathrm{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i \;=\; \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j} e^{z_j}} $$
Normalization keeps semantics (direction) and discards loudness (magnitude).
